You have been transported back in time (again!) this time to Enrico Fermi's office at the University of Chicago in 1942. Fermi is hard at work trying to build the first self-sustaining artificial nuclear reaction using Uranium 235. Once he calms down from the shock of witnessing time travel, Fermi has asked for your help to explore how factors such as size, shape and purity impact the ability of a sample of Uranium 235 to sustain a chain reaction.

Fermi initially presents you with his simplest model to help make it easy to troubleshoot and confirm the results from the Monte Carlo simulation that you will build of this chain reaction process. In this model, when a $^{235}U$ nucleus undergoes nuclear fission, it always releases two thermal neutrons, where a thermal neutron is one that is moving slowly enough to possibly be captured by other $^{235}U$ nuclei. These thermal neutrons will travel an average distance, known as the Mean Free Path $λ$, before being captured elsewhere in the sample. The probability of a thermal neutron being captured when it travels a distance $L$ is given by $p(L)∝e^{-L/λ}$. In this model, every captured thermal neutron results in an unstable $^{235}U$ nucleus which will almost immediately undergo its own fission process, releasing further neutrons, and possibly sustaining a chain reaction if enough of the neutrons are being captured inside the $^{235}U$ sample. You and Fermi have decided that a Monte Carlo process would be the best way to simulate this.

Your algorithm will look something like this:

  1. Choose an initial number of thermal neutrons, $N$, to simulate.
  2. Randomly generate $N$ starting locations within the Uranium for these initial thermal neutrons.
  3. Randomly generate the directions of travel, in three dimensions, of the $N$ initial thermal neutrons.
  4. Using the Mean Free Path, generate the distances, $L$, that the $N$ initial thermal neutrons will travel in the sample before being captured.
  5. Determine how many of the neutrons are absorbed inside the sample after they have each traveled their randomly determined distance $L$ in their randomly determined direction from their randomly determined initial position. Neutrons that are still inside the sample after traveling their distance $L$ are considered to have been captured and will initiate a new fission event. Neutrons that do not remain inside the sample after traveling their distance $L$ are lost and will not initiate any further fission events.
  6. Determine the number of new thermal neutrons generated, knowing that each of the new fission events will generate 2 new neutrons. We will call this total number of new thermal neutrons $N_{\text{new}}$.
  7. Report the multiplication factor, $f=N_{\text{new}}/N$, for that simulation. If this value is above 1, the number of thermal neutrons being generated is increasing, which will result in a sustained nuclear chain reaction.

def is_inside_cube(x, y, z, a):
    # check if neutrons is inside the cube with side length a
    return  (np.abs(x) <= a/2) & (np.abs(y) <= a/2) \
            & (np.abs(z) <= a/2)

def func(N):
    
    # N = Initial number of thermal neutrons
    N = 1000

    # Length of the cube's side
    a = 0.50 # m

    # Fermi's best guess of the mean free path
    # based on the neutron cross section in U235
    mean_free_path = .15 # m
    
    # Probability that an absorbed neutron results in a fission
    p = 1.0

    # Generate a random initial decay position for each neutron
    x0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    y0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    z0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)

    # Generate random initial directions for each neutron
    phi = 2*np.pi*(np.random.random(N))
    cos = 2*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    theta =  np.arccos(cos)

    # Randomly generate the distance travelled
    d = -mean_free_path*np.log(np.random.random(N))
    
    # Calculate the final positions
    x = x0 + d*np.sin(theta)*np.cos(phi)
    y = y0 + d*np.sin(theta)*np.sin(phi)
    z = z0 + d*np.cos(theta)
    
    # Count neutrons absorbed inside the cube
    absorbed = np.sum(is_inside_cube(x, y, z, a))
    
    # Each absorbed
    N_fissions = sum(np.random.random(absorbed)<p)
    N_new = 2.* N_fissions # 2 new neutrons per fission event
    
    # Calculate the multiplication factor
    f = N_new/N 
    return f

def simulate_fission(N, a, mean_free_path, shape, aspect_ratio=1):
    # The newly added aspect_ratio represents the ratio of height to diameter
    # a means the size of sample(initial state)
    
    # Initialize Neutrons
    x0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    y0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    z0 = a*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    phi = 2*np.pi*(np.random.random(N))
    cos = 2*(np.random.random(N)-.5)
    theta =  np.arccos(cos)
    d = -mean_free_path*np.log(np.random.random(N))
    
    # Calculate the final positions
    x = x0 + d*np.sin(theta)*np.cos(phi)
    y = y0 + d*np.sin(theta)*np.sin(phi)
    z = z0 + d*np.cos(theta)
    
    # Determining the shape of a sample
    if shape == "cube":
        inside = (np.abs(x) <= a/2) & (np.abs(y) <= a/2) \
                 & (np.abs(z) <= a/2)
        
    elif shape == "sphere":
        inside = (x**2 + y**2 + z**2) <= (a/2)**2
        
    elif shape == "cylinder": # Directed study 2
        inside = (x**2 + y**2 <= (a/2)**2) & (np.abs(z) <= a/2) # Assuming height is a
        
    elif shape == "cylinder2": # Directed study 3, 4
        # aspect_ratio =height/diameter
        initial_volume = np.pi * (a/2)**2*a 
        # Initial volume, assuming initial height equals diameter
        new_radius = np.cbrt(initial_volume / (np.pi*aspect_ratio*2))
        new_height = 2*new_radius * aspect_ratio
        inside = (x**2 + y**2 <= (new_radius)**2) & (np.abs(z) <= new_height/2)
        
    elif shape == "ellipsoid":
        # We only change the radius in the z-axis and assume that it remains a circle in the xy-plane.
        # aspect_ratio =long_axis/short_axis
        initial_volume = (4/3)*np.pi*(a/2)**3
        # Initial volume, assuming initial long_axis equals radius
        new_radius = np.cbrt(initial_volume / ((4/3) * np.pi * aspect_ratio))
        long_radius = new_radius*aspect_ratio
        inside = ((x**2 + y**2) / (new_radius)**2 + z**2 / (long_radius)**2) <= 1
        
    else: 
        raise ValueError("Invalid shape. Choose 'cube' or 'sphere' or 'cylinder' or 'cylinder2' or 'ellipsoid'.")
    
    # Count neutrons absorbed inside the simple
    absorbed = np.sum(inside)
    
    # Probability that an absorbed neutron results in a fission
    p = 1.0
    N_fissions = sum(np.random.random(absorbed)<p)
    
    # Average number of thermal neutrons
    N_therm_ave = 2.4355
    N_new = N_therm_ave * N_fissions 
    f = N_new/N 
    return f

def plot_Multiplication_Factor_volume(sample_sizes, shapes, N, mean_free_path, m_trials):
    results = []
    
    for shape in shapes:
        for size in sample_sizes:
            multiplication_factors = [] # Initialize here
            for _ in range(m_trials):
                multiplication_factors.append(simulate_fission(N, size, mean_free_path, shape, aspect_ratio=1))
            mean_f = np.mean(multiplication_factors)
            std_f = np.std(multiplication_factors)
            results.append({"Shape": shape, "Size": size, "Multiplication Factor": mean_f, "Error": std_f})
            
    results_df = pd.DataFrame(results)
    
    # Plotting
    fig, ax = plt.subplots()
    for shape in shapes:
        subset = results_df[results_df["Shape"] == shape]
        ax.errorbar(subset["Size"], subset["Multiplication Factor"] \
                        , yerr=subset["Error"], fmt = "o", capsize=5, label=f"Shape:{shape}")
        
        # Fitting the function
        yerror = subset["Error"]
        popt, _ = curve_fit(model, subset["Size"], subset["Multiplication Factor"], sigma=yerror, \
                            absolute_sigma=True)
        ax.plot(subset["Size"], model(subset["Size"], *popt), label=f"Fit: {shape}")

    ax.axhline(1, color="grey", linestyle="--")
    ax.set_xlabel("Sample Size (m)")
    ax.set_ylabel("Average Multiplication Factor")
    ax.set_title("Multiplication Factor vs Sample Size for Different Shapes")
    ax.legend()
    plt.show()

Synthesis and discussion¶

Research objectives Our study aims to investigate how three key aspects of nuclear physics simulations: volume and shape and aspect ratio affect multiplication factors.

Experimental Setup The study was carried out under consistent conditions: 1,000 initial neutrons, 1,000 experimental repetitions, and an average mean free path of 0.15 m. Here we also update the actual value of the decay, n = 2.4355. We focus on the multiplication factor performance of different shapes-cubes, cylinders, spheres (with decreasing volumes in that order)-and two shapes-ellipsoids, with cylinders varying their volumes and aspect ratios when the multiplication factor behaves.

The main findings Effect of volume change: A direct relationship was observed between volume increase and multiplication factor enhancement. The specific milestones at which the multiplication factor reaches 1 are approximately 0.2 meters for the cube size, 0.3 meters for the sphere size, and 0.6 meters for the cylinder size. (Here the height of the cylinder is equal to the diameter) A notable pattern is that the rate of increase of the multiplication factor levels off as the volume becomes sufficiently large. Effect of aspect ratio: An increase in the aspect ratio leads to a decrease in the multiplication factor for cylinders and ellipsoids at constant volume. This trend suggests that the shape of the material can have a significant effect on the multiplication factor even when the volume is constant. Quantitative analysis Since the initial linear model did not fully reflect the observed trends, a logarithmic growth model was used to fit the data. The model accurately reflected the stabilization of the multiplication factor with increasing volume, demonstrating its suitability for such complex simulations.

Limitations and future directions Physical modeling: Our model is based on idealized shape conditions and may not fully represent the complexity of real-world nuclear materials.
